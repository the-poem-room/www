Between Hours

Time has never been a strict thing,
not really.

In Iceland, the sun forgets
its bedtime —
morning lingers,
night take its time
deciding if it wants to arrive at all.
Hours stretch like curious arms,
reaching toward winter fireworks,
daylight stitched with quiet crackles,
a small daylight revolt,
the horizon unsure
what hour it holds.

Here at home
curtains closed,
the clock keeps its habits
but I don't always listen.
Pages fill, melodies spill,
one idea nudging the next,
thoughts tugging loose,
seconds loosening,
lost and briefly found,
a quiet countdown
dissolving into hush.

Time blurs
when I stop measuring it,
when the world outside
is simply allowed
to be wherever it is,
and I let myself be
right here,
in the honest hush
between heartbeats,
where light and thought
share the same soft space.

There's a kind of beauty
in forgetting the rules
we invented anyway:
that morning must be bright,
that evening must be dark,
that minutes must march
instead of dance —
instead of chance,
instead of slipping softly
out of step.

Maybe the most honest moments
are the ones that refuse
to find inside a calendar square,
the ones where a day
feels boundless,
where I float,
unbothered by ticking,
simply existing
in the soft stretch
between any beginning
and any ending,
the air still bending light,
whispering
that time is real
when we forget to care,
when we drift
between hours.

— Lilith
