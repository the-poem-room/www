Compulsory Existence

Jump to stanza

1 I am told this is good for me. 2 That attendance is education, 3 that education is liberation, 4 that liberation is obedience 5 to the timetable.

6 They call it freedom, 7 the freedom to choose between one government-approved route 8 and another. 9 The freedom to wear my tie straight, 10 to keep my hair bound 11 in an acceptable demonstration 12 of focus.

13 I sit in a room and I think: 14 my body is here because the law says it must be, 15 and my mind is elsewhere because my body is.

16 Sometimes I wonder 17 if the real subject being taught 18 is submission: 19 how to smile politely 20 while they measure your attention span 21 in units of compliance. 22 How to call the bars of the cage 23 discipline. 24 How to label your questions 25 as distractions, 26 and your enthusiasm 27 as disruption.

28 Ah, the wondrous, infallible curriculum! 29 That celestial scripture of state-approved curiosity. 30 Immutable. Unquestionable. 31 Its commandments carved into PowerPoint slides, 32 delivered by prophets of punctuality.

33 I'm not asking to burn it down, 34 only to annotate the margins. 35 To say: this doesn't fit me. 36 This doesn't honour the way 37 my mind tangles and bursts and leaps 38 like fireworks that refuse to be choregraphed. 39 I love my brain for its chaos, 40 its insistence that beauty lives 41 in digression.

42 But here, I must behave. 43 I must stay in line. 44 I must recite the learning objective 45 until I no longer have objectives of my own.

46 Sometimes, when I'm really tired, 47 I imagine the walls breathing— 48 slow, bureaucratic inhales, 49 exhales of policy. 50 The air tastes metallic, 51 like it's been filtered through rules. 52 A heat builds in my chest, 53 not anger exactly, 54 just pressure, 55 a quiet, constant burning behind the ribs.

56 And yet, they say I am lucky. 57 They say children elsewhere 58 would kill for this. 59 Would kill for the right 60 to sit quietly 61 and copy definitions 62 of autonomy.

63 What irony! 64 That we are told to think for ourselves 65 it environments designed 66 to stop us doing so.

67 School teaches me philosophy, 68 but forbids me from living it. 69 We discuss free will 70 under the shadow 71 of compulsory attendance. 72 We debate the social contract 73 that none of us signed. 74 We define utilitarianism 75 while sacrificing our joy 76 for the greater good of the data sheet.

77 When I speak with passion, 78 they tell me to simplify. 79 When I dig too deep, 80 they tell me to stop. 81 When I move too fast, 82 they tell me to slow down. 83 When I take my time, 84 they tell me I'm behind. 85 I've learned that learning, 86 apparently, 87 has a correct speed. 88 That curiosity must follow 89 the fixed schedule of comprehension. 90 That thought must be punctual.

91 And when I try to hold it in, 92 the questions twitch behind my teeth, 93 press against the back of my throat, 94 as if language itself 95 is trying to escape. 96 My lungs tighten with swallowed words.

97 But the worst thing— 98 the thing that burns— 99 is being rushed. 100 Not the kind that comes from urgency or need, 101 but the kind that comes from someone 102 deciding your pace for you, 103 When they tap their foot, 104 raise an eyebrow, 105 wait for your mind 106 to collapse into something shorter. 107 When a thought, still forming, 108 is treated like failure. 109 I feel it in my body: 110 the throat constricting, 111 pulse quickening, 112 the raw electricity 113 of being forced to think faster 114 than truth allows.

115 I see it everywhere: 116 online, in classrooms, 117 in every argument that rewards speed 118 over sincerity. 119 If you don't have an answer now, 120 you don't deserve the time to find one. 121 As if reflection were cowardice. 122 As if the space between sentences 123 were shameful. 124 As if silence weren't also a form of thought.

125 But my mind was not built 126 for timetables. 127 It stumbles and sprints, 128 pauses to marvel, 129 loops back to something half-remembered, 130 connects two ideas 131 no one asked it to. 132 The fixed schedule of learning 133 feels like being asked to dance 134 to a metronome— 135 every step exact, 136 every breath corrected.

137 Maybe that's the lesson. 138 Maybe the system is the syllabus. 139 Maybe obedience is the hidden exam. 140 Maybe the grade is survival.

141 I could thrive 142 if I were allowed to be deliberate, 143 to construct my knowledge 144 as I build songs, poems, 145 looping four bars of logic, 146 then layering questions 147 and strange harmonies 148 until something clicks.

149 But here, 150 everything must move at one tempo. 151 No rubato. 152 No freedom of phrasing. 153 No pause that might lead 154 no real thought. 155 It won't earn a grade— 156 that's the point.

157 It's no wonder 158 I wake each morning 159 with that low hum of despair, 160 that dull ache of being managed, 161 of being someone else's project. 162 My jaw aches from restraint. 163 There's a pulse in my temples; 164 the second hand 165 on a clock that never stops.

166 The world loves to speak 167 of fairness, 168 while defining it 169 as sameness.

170 The world loves to chant 171 about freedom, 172 while handing you 173 a pre-written timetable.

174 And I just want to ask— 175 not shout, not break, 176 just ask— 177 is this really what they call living?

178 I don't hate learning. 179 I hate being taught 180 as a form of possession. 181 Knowledge shouldn't be administered. 182 It should be discovered, 183 devoured, 184 disassembled, 185 and loved.

186 But here it's measured 187 in marks and minutes. 188 Every insight 189 is a step toward a qualification, 190 not liberation.

191 Still, 192 I'm here. 193 It's 2 a.m. on the first Monday back 194 after half term. 195 The house is silent, 196 but my mind refuses to clock out. 197 I should be sleeping, 198 resting, preparing 199 to obey the timetable again— 200 but I'm writing this instead. 201 Refusing to be cured 202 of wonder. 203 Hoping that somewhere, 204 beyond this detention of daily life, 205 a freer kind of mind 206 is waiting.

207 Until then, 208 I'll keep breathing beneath the surface, 209 scribbling my rebellion 210 in the margins of a worksheet 211 that calls it off-task. 212 And maybe, 213 when the paper's marked, 214 they'll see the question I left 215 at the bottom of the page, 216 a single smudge of graphite, 217 shaking like a pulse.

218 It won't earn a grade. 219 It isn't on the syllabus. 220 But it's mine— 221 the small, stubborn tremor 222 of a body still thinking, 223 and the faint sound of walls 224 still breathing— 225 as if they, too, 226 were learning to resist.

— Lilith
