The Second Zapruder

Jump to stanza

1 The podium grins, 2 its plastic smile lit fluorescent white. 3 "Do you know how many mass shooters there have been in America over the last 10 years?" 4 he's asked. 5 He leans into the mic— 6 "Counting or not counting gang violence?" he quips, 7 and before the applause can land, 8 the punctuation arrives.

9 A sound sharper than his slogans. 10 Irony delivered faster than thought. 11 The banner behind him trembles, 12 patriotic fabric swallowing his shadow whole.

13 Phones rise like communion wafers, 14 history live-streamed. 15 Hearts and fire emojis flicker, 16 digital rosaries clutched in panic and glee. 17 Already, the clip is immortal— 18 frame by frame dissected, 19 the Zapruder film of our generation, 20 but drained of gravity, 21 bloated with absurdity.

22 And while his body chools behind the podium, 23 across town glass shatters in a classroom. 24 Children press against the floor, 25 their backpacks blooming crimson. 26 One minute, two headlines, 27 but only one story commands the spotlight.

28 How American: 29 that bullets finished sentences 30 debate never could. 31 How predictable: 32 that extremism's sharpest dialect 33 is still gunfire.

34 But hear this clearly: 35 Charlie Kirk was horrid— 36 a man who traded empathy for venom, 37 who feasted on division. 38 His death is not justice. 39 It is a mirror, 40 showing us what we have become: 41 a country rehearsed in killing, 42 fluent in shrugging, 43 drunk on the myth that violence 44 is a form of truth.

45 The tragedy is not his silence, 46 but our acceptance. 47 Not the fall of one despicable man, 48 but the ritual of blood answering speech, 49 of children's screams dimmed beneath applause.

50 Another villain. 51 Another victim. 52 Another classroom erased. 53 And the country shrugs again, 54 as if the anthem itself 55 were scored in gunfire.

56 The blood dries quickly, 57 but the irony does not.

— Lilith
