Trying to Bite Your Own Teeth

Jump to stanza

1 i'm so 2 unsure

3 whether uncertainty is wisdom 4 is jut fear 5 with a reading list.

6 I envy people who seem able 7 to arrive at conclusions without circling them 8 for three hours first. 9 People who say things like 10 "this is obviously wrong" 11 and apparently sleep perfectly well afterwards.

12 Which is interesting, because 13 I use words like "obviously" too.

14 I wrote "cruelty" earlier 15 without hesitation.

16 It's strange 17 how quickly my uncertainty disappears 18 around suffering.

19 Then I spend fifteen minutes 20 choosing between two moisturisers 21 as though one of them 22 contains the morally correct way 23 to exist inside a body.

24 Sometimes I stand completely still 25 in supermarket aisles 26 because every choice suddenly branches outward 27 into consequences I cannot fully calculate.

28 Factory farms. 29 Packaging. 30 Rainforests. 31 Advertising. 32 My own hypocrisy. 33 Whether wanting anything at all 34 already compromises the whole exercise.

35 Meanwhile somebody nearby 36 has already chosen cereal 37 without accidentally questioning 38 the structure of reality.

39 Or worse, I do know how I feel, 40 and the feeling arrives first 41 while the explanation rushes after it 42 buttoning its shirt.

43 Split-brain patients do things 44 and then the brain invests a reason 45 smooth enough to trust.

46 I think about that constantly.

47 How many of my beliefs 48 were feelings first?

49 How many arguments 50 are just emotions 51 with better grammar?

52 And if that sentence felt convincing, 53 was that because it was true 54 or because my brain enjoys symmetry?

55 Love appears. 56 Fear appears. 57 Disgust. Tenderness.

58 I never consciously select any of them.

59 They simply arrive 60 already carrying names.

61 Maybe morality does too.

62 I still recoil from screams 63 before philosophy arrives.

64 I've spent entire evenings 65 trying to untangle convinction 66 from instinct, 67 then trying to untangle instinct 68 from social conditioning, 69 then trying to determine 70 whether the distinction even exists.

71 At some point 72 the whole thing starts folding in on itself.

73 Something ancient in me 74 still hears rustling grass 75 and prepares for teeth.

76 Which probably kept somebody alive once.

77 Truth may have been incidental.

78 And then I realise 79 the thing producing all this doubt 80 is the exact same organ 81 I'm trying to doubt.

82 A brain examing itself 83 feels a bit like trying to bite your own teeth.

84 Or trying to look directly 85 at your own eyes 86 without a mirror.

87 SOmetimes I genuinely think 88 everyone else received 89 some fundamental instruction manual 90 that I misplaced immediately.

91 The world keeps telling me 92 to follow my heart.

93 My heart is tissue and electricity.

94 It reacts before "I" do.

95 Maybe certainty does too.

96 Bertrand Russell wrote 97 that stupid people are full of certainty 98 while intelligent people are full of doubt.

99 In ordinary life 100 that often feels true.

101 Science works.

102 We can predict eclipses centuries in advance, 103 photograph black holes, 104 send signals into space 105 and receive information back 106 from things older than language.

107 Every sunrise 108 feels persuasive.

109 That bothers me.

110 Because persuasion isn't proof.

111 Pattern is not proof. 112 Habit is not proof. 113 Memory is not proof.

114 And still I set alarms 115 as though tomorrow 116 has signed a contract.

117 I buy groceries 118 like the future 119 is guaranteed to arrive.

120 The assumptions work 121 with terrifying consistency.

122 Enough consistency 123 that doubting them too much 124 starts to resemble illness.

125 WHich is maybe why 126 almost nobody does it all the way.

127 Sometimes I think 128 if I questioned every assumption 129 with complete sincerity 130 I would never leave bed again.

131 Or speak.

132 Or tust language enough 133 to finish a sentence.

134 The strange things is that society mostly functions 135 because people pretend they know enough.

136 Doctors. 137 Teachers. 138 Parents. 139 Scientists.

140 Me.

141 Especially me.

142 I keep writing sentences 143 that sound definitive 144 in a poem about uncertainty.

145 I don't read philosophy 146 expecting answers anymore.

147 Mostly I read it 148 the way people watch demolition videos.

149 One argument 150 kicking holes through another.

151 And then another arriving 152 to kick holes through that one too.

153 Sometimes I lie awake wondering 154 whether rationality 155 is just an extremely sophisticated form 156 of guessing correctly often enough.

157 Or whether that sentence itself 158 only feels intelligent 159 because I've heard variations of it before.

160 I say "I" constantly 161 despite not understanding 162 what that word refers to.

163 Am I a consciousness 164 looking outward at a world beyond itself?

165 Or part of the world 166 mistakenly experiencing separation 167 because separation happened to be useful?

168 I don't know how I could verify either.

169 And even that statement 170 quietly assumes 171 verification is possible.

172 Maybe consciousness produces reality. 173 Maybe reality produces consciousness.

174 Maybe both ideas 175 are embarrassingly human.

176 Maybe the need to choose between them is too.

177 I reach for a glass of water 178 before becoming aware 179 of deciding to reach for it.

180 The songs somebody listened to 181 at fourteen until two in the morning. 182 The exact tone a person used once 183 that still reappears years later 184 without permission. 185 Being called "too sensitive" 186 often enough 187 that it starts sounding medical.

188 Apparently large sections of my brain 189 have stopped keeping me informed.

190 But guilt still exists somehow.

191 Regret too.

192 I still replay conversations 193 from years ago 194 like my brain is trying to revise history 195 through sheer repetition.

196 Maybe certainty is unavoidable.

197 Or maybe I only say that 198 because uncertainty 199 becomes exhausting after enough years.

200 Sometimes a chord progression resolves 201 in exactly the right way 202 and my body believes it 203 before my mind has time 204 to object.

205 Then five minutes later 206 I'm back to questioning 207 whether meaning itself 208 is only another feeling 209 arriving too quickly.

210 Sometimes somebody speaks gently to me 211 and for a few seconds 212 doubt stops talking.

213 Not permanently. 214 Just long enough 215 for me to notice the silence.

216 Walking home through cold streets 217 with music in my headphones 218 feeling almost unearably grateful 219 that consciousness exists at all.

220 Then immediately wondering 221 whether gratitude is just dopamine 222 misinterpreting itself poetically.

223 None of those feelings were chosen either.

224 They happened to me.

225 I care about frighten animals 226 in a way that genuinely hurts sometimes.

227 We can map distant galaxies 228 while building industrial systems 229 designed around screaming.

230 I notice 231 I never put quotation marks 232 around suffering.

233 Maybe that means something.

234 Or maybe pain only feels morally obvious 235 because evolution made social creatures 236 that way.

237 Or maybe saying that 238 already feels vaguely monstrous.

239 I honestly can't tell anymore.

240 Maybe this is intellectual honesty.

241 Or maybe I'm just afraid 242 of becoming somebody 243 who mistakes confidence 244 for understanding.

245 Tomorrow 246 I will probably speak confidently again.

247 I'll say 248 "that's cruel" 249 or "that's beautiful" 250 or "I know."

251 And part of me 252 will believe it completely.

253 Another part 254 will immediately begin 255 interrogating the sentence.

256 And another part 257 will become suspicious 258 of the interrogation too.

259 i'm still 260 unsure

— Lilith
