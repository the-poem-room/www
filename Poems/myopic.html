Myopic

1 We built telescopes 2 capable of photographing 3 the afterglow of creation itself — 4 ancient light crossing thirteen billion years 5 of expanding darkness 6 simply to arrive here, 7 to astonishment.

8 Yet children now learn 9 the taste of wildfire smoke 10 before algebra.

11 Myopia is not blindness.

12 It is the refusal 13 to look long enough.

14 We looked outward 15 before learning how to look ahead.

16 So we measure existence 17 in quarters and percentages: 18 stock markets climbing 19 with the temperature, 20 election cycles compressing history 21 into four-year fragments 22 while glaciers loosen themselves 23 into dark water.

24 The species that named quasars 25 still cannot imagine a world 26 not powered by burning things.

27 Summer arrives incorrectly now.

28 October wearing April's temperature. 29 Bees waking too early 30 into air with nothing left to pollinate. 31 Birdsong thinning at dawn. 32 The trees uncertain 33 when to sleep

34 And somewhere a minister appears on television 35 speaking calmly 36 about economic necessity 37 while a teacher repairs a classroom globe 38 with tape peeling at the equator.

39 Children learn branding 40 before ecology. 41 Advertisements interrupt documentaries 42 about extinction. 43 Consumption staged beneath artificial light. 44 Perfect fruit crosses oceans 45 to rot untouched in supermarket bins.

46 Outside, 47 reservoirs shrink into cracked geometry. 48 The asphalt softens in heat. 49 Forests breathe smoke for weeks at a time, 50 their blackened trunks standing upright 51 like a language 52 nobody translated quickly enough.

53 Entire species vanish quietly — 54 not with cinematic catastrophe, 55 but with fewer wings against windows 56 each passing summer.

57 Summer nights grow quieter each year. 58 Even the moths seem unconvinced 59 by the light.

60 Still, the scientists continue 61 in careful voices: 62 graph after graph, 63 equation after equation, 64 trying to translate collapse 65 into something governments might finally hear.

66 Not conquerors. 67 Not prophets. 68 Just people disciplined enough 69 to let reality contradict them.

70 Science — 71 the sound of certainty 72 learning its limits.

73 The universe remains larger 74 than ideology, 75 larger than markets, 76 larger than the childish belief 77 that consumption can continue forever 78 simply because spreadsheets say it should.

79 We discovered 80 that every atom in our blood 81 was forged inside stellar collapse.

82 We measured the temperatures 83 of distant stars 84 while our own oceans quietly developed fevers. 85 Because to understand a thing deeply 86 should mark it harder to destroy.

87 Instead, 88 economies devour tomorrow 89 to keep today comfortable. 90 We drill deeper into oceans. 91 Clear ancient forests 92 for grazing land and highways. 93 Wrap convenience in layers of plastic 94 designed to outlive their usefulness 95 by centuries. 96 Teach children competition 97 before coexistence.

98 Cargo ships drag entire cities of light 99 across black water at midnight. 100 Data centres hum all night 101 to sustain our appetite 102 for distraction.

103 The future has always suffered 104 from a lack of voting rights.

105 Perhaps that is the truest form 106 of human myopia: 107 mistaking immediacy for importance.

108 We scroll through wildfires 109 with our thumbs. 110 Watch floodwater carrying bicycles, 111 photographs, 112 children's toys. 113 Order disposable comforts 114 while coral reefs bleach ghost-white 115 beneath warming tides.

116 Not monsters.

117 Just clever animals 118 fatally addicted 119 to short-term comfort.

120 This is what makes it unbearable.

121 We know.

122 We have seen the Earth 123 from the distance of the moon — 124 borderless, blue, 125 fragile enough 126 to vanish behind a human thumb.

127 Astronauts returned speechless from that vision. 128 Scientists spent decades 129 trying to hand us the feeling intact — 130 not merely the data, 131 but the humility.

132 Look, they said. 133 Look how small this is. 134 Look how rare.

135 But nations still argue 136 over whose responsibility it is 137 to stop the house from burning.

138 Meanwhile cities breathe 139 through smoke-filtered lungs. 140 Even the rain feels industrial now.

141 And still, somewhere tonight, 142 a child balances a cheap telescope 143 against a bedroom window 144 while heat lightning flickers silently 145 above distant fields.

146 She learns 147 that galaxies collide slowly 148 that light survives impossible distances, 149 that curiosity itself 150 is a form of moral courage.

151 Then she lowers the telescope.

152 And for the first time, 153 really looks 154 at the tree outside her house — 155 half its leaves missing in July, 156 one branch dead from the inside out, 157 ants carrying pale eggs upward 158 as floodwater gathers in the roots.

159 Tomorrow, in school, 160 she will still be taught 161 how economies grow.

162 But nobody will explain 163 why the bees are disappearing.

164 Nobody will tell her 165 that the great tragedy of our species 166 was never ignorance.

167 Only the failure 168 to let knowledge change us.

169 We were simply too small-souled 170 for the size of our knowledge.

171 And somewhere above her, 172 the stars continue expanding 173 in perfect silence, 174 without needing us 175 to survive.

— Lilith
