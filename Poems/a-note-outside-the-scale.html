A Note Outside the Scale

I am not a man,
nor a woman—
nor half of one pretending to be whole.
I am GNP:
a balance, an absence,
a body that speaks in shifting pitches,
sometimes velvet-deep,
sometimes bright as flutes.

My hair is clean, tied, blonde—
a kind of rebellion against the buzz-cut of expectation.
My voice is a compass,
shifting with the room,
with the ears
that would try to name me.

The world of hetero eyes
is a hall of mirrors.
Every glance a ranking,
every conversation a ledger
ticking "hot or not,"
measured by rules
that collapse when touched.
Boys: praised for conquest.
Girls: chastised unless
they are both unbroken porcelain
and marked by use.
A cruel arithmetic
I refuse to play.

But with lesbians,
I breathe.
They do not script me
into a part I never auditioned for.
They hear my laughter,
not its register.
They see my body
without turning it into battlefield:
what I should be,
who should I want,
how many I should have won.

Perhaps they know—
from living outside the story
that was written without them—
that freedom feels like
an unscored passage.
That kindness can grow
where expectation does not root.

With lesbians, I am treated better,
because they do not mistake me
for a symbol of their desire
or a soldier of tradition.
They meet me where I am:
a voice that wavers,
a body I keep clean,
a mind hungry for ideas
a soul refusing the ledger.

And in their presence,
I am not unfinished,
not miscast,
not half.
I am exactly enough—
a note outside the scale
that makes music.

— Lilith
